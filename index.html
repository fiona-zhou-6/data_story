Illinois’ shrinking licensed practical nurse workforce deserves closer attention

Licensing data within the past five years have shown the number of in-state licensed practical nurses and registered nurse counts have declined, pointing towards a trend that could affect workforce planning and access to care within the state.

With an increasing national population of older adults, more people are now relying on the nursing workforce to provide accessible care. Photo credit: Unsplash free stock photo.

With Illinois’ aging population of baby boomers and retirement, the nursing workforce responsible for providing hands-on care for patients is beginning to shrink. Recent data from the Illinois Nursing Workforce Center has indicated a decrease in overall registered nurses since 2020.

According to a STAT news article published in 2022, the nursing workforce has seen a national shortage in workers since COVID. Workforce experts have raised concerns that nurse shortages are not only tied to the supply of licensed nurses, but also burnout and working conditions.

Within Illinois, state licensing data has shown a decrease in the number of in-state registered nurse and licensed practical nurse (LPN) holders over the past five years, raising questions about whether the state is prepared to address the needs of growing long-term care.

Based on recent licensing data from the Illinois Nursing Workforce Center (INWC), in-state LPN license holders have fallen by 12.8%, compared with a decline among registered license holders of about 4.9%.

However, the decline among license holders is not universal. Advanced practice license nurses have seen significant growth during this same period, suggesting that Illinois’ nursing workforce is continuously shifting.

An earlier report in 2020 conducted by INWC predicted a shortage of nearly 15,000 registered nurses (RN) by 2025, with a later report stating that 27% of the workforce planned to retire in the next five years and 41% planning to leave the profession in the next 10 years.

Registered nurses generally have more advanced training and broader responsibilities to treat patients and create care plans, while LPN provide essential, long-term care and rehabilitation  under the supervision of RNs.

A decline in either workforce can create a strain on the health care system, especially for LPN roles in non-hospital care settings. For Illinois workforce planners, maintaining and continuously recruiting LPNs has become important as older adults begin requiring more care outside of traditional hospital settings.

The challenge is further complicated by the current LPN workforce. The 2025 INWC report found that 35% of Illinois LPNs were 55 or older, while only 17% were younger than 34.

Despite the share of older LPNs declining since 2019, the data still suggests that the  workforce has a small pipeline of younger nurses entering into the profession behind those approaching retirement age.

The decline among RN license holders remains steep with a net loss in state of 8393 workers between the past five years and 3221 workers for LPN license holders. RNs declined by a smaller share compared to LNs due to their larger workforce, but LPNs still present a larger raw decrease.

By 2025, Illinois’ in-state LPN license count was down to 87.2% of its 2020 level, while RNs license count was down to 95.1%.

These findings point towards a need for further attention into supporting not only registered nurses and licensed practical nurses within Illinois, but nursing occupations nationwide as a whole to support the increasing gap between supply and demand between patients and health care providers.

Methodology and Fact-checking

Inspiration for this story came from a WBEZ Chicago story on Endeavor Health nurses unionizing over working conditions and pay cuts. A survey from NBC gave details and information on over 1178 patient responses around the Chicago area based on whether or not they believed they struggled to get access to health and treatment within the past year.

After a further look into data from the Illinois Nursing Workforce Center and analyzing active nursing licenses within the past five years, there was significant data to show that there has been a decrease in license holders for registered nurses and licensed practical nurses since 2020. The data was split into separate data tables and then manually compiled into a spreadsheet that recorded license type, fiscal year, in-state license holders, out-of-state license holders, and total license holders. There were a few mismatched findings within the dataset, but all of those were noted in the spreadsheet and double checked against the data within the documents given by the department. All numbers were ultimately selected and chosen based on the data provided.

After cleaning the spreadsheet, it was converted into a comma separated variable dataset and exploratory analysis was conducted using Python in VSCode using basic pandas and numpy tools. From there, net change and percent change for each license type was calculated by comparing 2025 counts with 2020 counts. For Full Practice Authority APRNs, 2021was the baseline year because the early 2020 data still remained transitional and not as updated.

The analysis of data focused mainly on in-state registered nurses and licensed practical nurse license holders because these were the two groups that showed decline over the five year period. Additionally summary tables were created to make comparisons easier. Then, to analyze RN and LPN specifically, certain columns and rows were selected for Flourish visualizations.
